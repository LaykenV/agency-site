Before You Dial

Three Things to Convey Early

  1. You're local. "I'm based in Youngsville — happy to drive up to Alexandria if it gets serious." Louisiana credibility matters.
  2. You build custom sites, not templates. "I do custom-designed sites for local businesses. Not a template shop." Differentiates from RealGeeks.
  3. You're curious, not selling. "I'd rather understand your situation than pitch you anything generic." Lowers her guard, gets her talking.

Discovery Questions (In Priority Order)

1"How did you end up on RealGeeks? What were you looking for when you signed up?"
Learn her original intent + how long she's been on the platform. Tells you whether she's evaluated alternatives.
2"How's it working for you right now? What do you love, what frustrates you?"
The most important question. Shut up and listen. Don't agree or disagree — just take notes. Pain = opportunity.
3"How are your 16 agents actually using the site day-to-day?"
Reveals whether the team actually adopts the CRM/IDX features or just uses their phone. Critical for sizing the CRM tier.
4"Where do most of your leads come from? IDX searches? Referrals? Walk-ins?"
Lead source mix tells you what to optimize. If 80% are referrals, IDX matters less. If 80% are organic search, SEO matters most.
5"How are you tracking and following up on leads right now? Happy with the CRM piece?"
This decides Path A vs Path B. If she's frustrated → Path B (FUB). If she's fine → Path A (Wise Agent).
6"If you had to name the single biggest frustration with your current setup, what would it be?"
Forces her to prioritize. Whatever she says is the headline for your proposal.
7"Have you been thinking about a change for a while, or is something specific bringing this up now?"
Reveals urgency. "Just exploring" = low priority. "Contract is up in March" = real timing.
8"If you could wave a wand and have the perfect setup, what would it actually do for you?"
Aspirational. Gets her dreaming. Whatever she lists is what you reference back in the proposal.
9"Have you talked to any other web designers about this?"
Tells you who you're competing with. If she says "Luxury Presence quoted me $15k" — you know your ceiling.
10"Besides yourself, who else needs to weigh in on a decision like this?"
Find the decision-makers. If Rusty (broker) has veto power, you need him on call #2.

What NOT To Do

Avoid all of these on this call

  • Don't quote prices. Even if she asks twice. "I'll send a proposal" is the right answer.
  • Don't promise specific features. "I can do X" without scoping is how projects blow up.
  • Don't trash-talk RealGeeks. She picked it for a reason. Stay neutral.
  • Don't pretend to be a real estate expert. Honesty about being new to the vertical builds trust.
  • Don't pitch your $199/mo WaaS. Wrong product for her. Don't even mention it.
  • Don't get pulled into a technical deep-dive. "I'll cover that in the proposal" is fine.
  • Don't push for a decision today. First-call closes don't happen on $10k+ projects.
  • Don't agree to scope you can't deliver. "Let me think about that and get back to you" is allowed.

Take Notes During the Call

Yellow Flags — Slow Down

Reasons to be cautious (not deal-breakers, but reconsider)

  • She's in months 1–6 of a 12-month RealGeeks contract. Two-month buyout doesn't make sense for her. Suggest revisiting at month 9.
  • She wants you to also manage CRM, drip campaigns, paid ads, social media. Out of scope for you. Reset expectations or pass.
  • Her agents are dug in / will resist migration. Migration nightmare. Make sure she has the political capital to push it through.
  • She has a budget ceiling under $3k for the build. Won't work for a 16-agent brokerage. Politely decline.
  • She's "just shopping around" with no specific motivation. Low close probability. Send proposal but don't over-invest.
  • She expects you to also run Facebook/Google ads. Not your offer. Decline that piece explicitly.
  • She wants the new site to keep using RealGeeks behind the scenes. Won't work technically. Re-explain or walk.
